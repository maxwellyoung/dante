# Thirty Flights of Loving — Reference Brain

> "Thirty Flights of Loving tells a better story in 13 minutes than most games do in 13 hours." — Graham Smith, PC Gamer

---

## 1. Overview

- **Developer:** Brendon Chung / Blendo Games (solo)
- **Release:** August 2012 (Win), November 2012 (Mac), December 2021 (Linux)
- **Engine:** Modified id Tech 2 (Quake II) — KMQuake II port by Knightmare, Lazarus mod by David Hyde / Mad Dog
- **Runtime:** ~13 minutes
- **Price:** $5 at launch (Steam, itch.io)
- **Genre:** First-person short story / narrative experiment
- **Predecessor:** Gravity Bone (2008), free, bundled on Steam
- **Awards:** Nominated Excellence in Narrative (IGF 2013), honorable mentions for Seumas McNally Grand Prize and Nuovo Award
- **Source code:** Open on GitHub (`blendogames/thirtyflightsofloving`)

Chung chose the Quake II engine despite having access to newer tools — because it was open-source, freely redistributable, and he'd modded it for a decade. The constraints became the aesthetic.

---

## 2. Narrative Structure

### The Story
A heist gone wrong. Three criminals: you, Anita (your girlfriend), Borges (your best friend). The game covers planning, execution, and catastrophic aftermath — fragmented across time.

### Non-Linear Editing as Core Mechanic
The game does not proceed chronologically. It jump-cuts freely between past, present, and future. Bar planning the heist → airport mid-escape → wedding flashback → mid-gunfight. The player assembles narrative emotionally, not logically.

### Elliptical Storytelling
Massive gaps. You never see the heist itself. You never hear dialogue. The game values mood and impression over coherent plot.

> "The narrative is both elliptical and nonlinear, jumping forward and back in time while leaving confusing gaps in its story, valuing style and mood over leaving the player with a coherent idea of the events."

### No Dialogue, No Cutscenes
Every piece of information is communicated visually — objects on tables, posters on walls, clothing, spatial relationships. The player reads the world, not text.

### The Jump Cut as Interactive Device
The game physically teleports you from one scene to the next. Walk through a door → different place, different time. Not a loading screen or fade-to-black — an instantaneous spatial and temporal cut. The interactive equivalent of Godard's jump cuts in Breathless.

### What Gets Cut
Everything a traditional game would make you play through. Walking A to B? Cut. The boring middle of a gunfight? Cut. Hours of waiting? A clock above a door jumps forward and your companions appear with wedding gifts.

> "While cinema edits out the time when nothing or unimportant action is taking place, video games typically show everything and the whole journey." — PopMatts

Thirty Flights refuses this convention.

## 3. Visual Design

### The Blocky Aesthetic
Skinny characters with blocky rectangular heads. Deliberately non-realistic — lo-fi expressionism where abstraction forces the player to project emotion. The sophistication of the storytelling against the crudeness of the rendering IS the point.

### Engine Constraints as Aesthetic
Quake II: simple BSP geometry, low-poly models, flat-shaded surfaces. Within these constraints, Chung builds spaces that feel warm, specific, and authored.

### Color Palette
Pinks, reds, oranges, blues. Warm and vibrant. Influenced by:
- **Wong Kar-wai** (Christopher Doyle as DP) — dreamlike saturated colors of Fallen Angels, Chungking Express
- **Godard** — bold primaries of Pierrot le Fou (red-white-blue)

### The Contrast
Narrative ambition has nothing to do with visual fidelity. A 1997 engine can tell a story that AAA games with millions of polygons cannot.

---

## 4. Level Design

### Key Spaces
- **The bar/hideout:** Under a bar. Ammunition, fake passports, plans, alcohol. Intimate. Establishes the criminal world and relationships.
- **The airport:** The escape attempt. Larger, more open. The moment everything falls apart.
- **The heist location:** Glimpsed in fragments, never fully shown.
- **The wedding/party:** Flashback spaces establishing emotional stakes.

### Theme Park Design Philosophy
Chung explicitly cites **Disneyland** as influence:

> Theme parks must be "aesthetically gorgeous spaces infused with story and character while also having logistical considerations like being suitable for people to walk through and understandable so people don't get lost."

Beauty + utilitarianism. The player is never lost, never wonders what to do.

### Tight-to-Open Rhythm
Corridors compress into larger spaces. Architecture guides without signposting. Spaces feel lived-in despite geometric simplicity.

---

## 5. Pacing and Rhythm

### Rhythm Over Length
The game understands pacing as rhythm, not duration. Editing does more storytelling than mechanics.

### Musical Rhythm in Action
The gunfight scene was designed with "musical rhythm" — inspired by Koyaanisqatsi and Baraka (Godfrey Reggio / Ron Fricke). Rhythmic montage, non-narrative.

### Cutting the Boring Parts
A gunfight → reduced to its impression. Hours of waiting → collapsed into a clock jump. Absolute respect for the player's time.

### Disorientation as Tool
> "Transitions from one scene to another can be abrupt and jarring, a deliberate part of the technique that accentuates its effectiveness through disorientation and a sharpening of focus."

### Trust
> "It doesn't explain itself, it doesn't wait for you, and it certainly doesn't care if you're confused. In fact, confusion is the point."

---

## 6. Sound Design

- **Composer:** Chris Remo (Idle Thumbs, later Campo Santo / Firewatch)
- **Score:** 4 tracks, ~7 minutes. Latin American-influenced.
- **Additional audio:** Jared Emerson-Johnson, A.J. LoCascio. Sound library from Soundsnap.
- **Function:** Music bridges scenes across jump cuts. Provides continuity where visuals deliberately fragment. Soundtrack carries emotional information that stripped-down visuals and absent dialogue cannot.

---

## 7. Game Design Philosophy

### What Is Stripped Away
- No health bar
- No shooting (pick up guns, never use them)
- No fail states
- No puzzles
- No dialogue or text exposition
- No player death
- Minimal interaction — walking + occasionally picking up objects

### What Remains
Movement through space. Observation. Being present in a scene. The player's role is to **witness**, not overcome.

### Subversion of FPS Conventions
Built on a literal FPS engine. Uses FPS muscle memory and spatial expectations, then redirects entirely toward narrative. You move like a shooter protagonist but you are a **detective of atmosphere**.

## 8. Cinematic Influences

### Jean-Luc Godard / French New Wave
- **Breathless (1960):** The original jump cut. Godard cut "boring" parts of continuous shots, creating jarring temporal leaps. Thirty Flights does this at the scene level.
- **Pierrot le Fou (1965):** Explicitly cited. Bold primaries, crime narrative, romance, non-linear structure.
- **Godard's editing philosophy:** "The space between the cuts suggests a wealth of information." What is NOT shown is as important as what is.

### Wong Kar-wai
- **Fallen Angels, Chungking Express:** Dreamlike visuals, fragmented narratives, saturated color, romantic melancholy.
- **Christopher Doyle** (cinematographer): Handheld, intimate, color-drenched.

### Koyaanisqatsi / Baraka
- Rhythmic montage, non-narrative structure. The gunfight's "musical rhythm."

### The Key Innovation
**"First-person cutting"** — applying cinematic editing to an interactive first-person perspective. Games had used cutscenes and camera changes before. Thirty Flights cuts WITHIN the player's continuous first-person experience. You are not watching a movie — you are **inside a movie being edited around you**.

---

## 9. Cultural Impact

### Pioneer of the "Walking Simulator"
Alongside Dear Esther (2012), The Stanley Parable (2011/2013), and Gone Home (2013) — "the tip of the spear" for the 3D exploration genre in 2012-2013.

### Direct Descendants
- **Virginia (2016):** Explicitly inspired by Thirty Flights' jump-cut technique. Narrative mystery told entirely through first-person jump cuts, no dialogue.
- **Paratopic (2018):** "Draws ideas from Thirty Flights of Loving but takes them somewhere terrible." PS1-aesthetic horror using the same cinematic editing language.

**Lineage:** Thirty Flights → Virginia → Paratopic

### Broader Influence
Proved that narrative ambition and visual fidelity are unrelated. A 13-minute game can be as culturally significant as a 40-hour one. Cinematic language translates to interactive media without cutscenes.

## 10. Key Quotes

> "Only the moments that matter survive to tell a story that's powerful despite its brevity. [It] does a better job illuminating its characters in 15 minutes containing no real speech than Square-Enix can manage in a whole series' worth of voiced half-hour cutscenes." — Gamecritics

> "Like the very best narratives, Thirty Flights of Loving relies on economy more than excess, and it races you breathlessly to its conclusion rather than herding you through an awkward gauntlet of false choices and bottlenecks."

> "The sort of game Edgar Wright might make." — PC Gamer

> "The intervening hours weren't important to the story, and consequently players don't have to spend the time experiencing them, while the experience remains continuous even if the fiction is not." — PopMatts

## 12. Mapping to Endearing Void

| Thirty Flights | Endearing Void |
|---|---|
| Quake II engine (1997) | Raylib + pure C, 480x300 PS1 aesthetic |
| Godard / Wong Kar-wai palette | Godard palette (French red, French blue, warm whites) |
| 13 minutes, every second authored | Short-form, every moment matters |
| No dialogue, environmental storytelling | Minimal writing, "three hours to kill" throughline |
| Jump cuts between spaces/times | Scene transitions (taxi → hotel → room → balcony → stars) |
| Theme park wayfinding | Guided exploration through hotel architecture |
| Lo-fi visuals, sophisticated narrative | PS1 aesthetic, cinematic ambition |
| Walk and observe, no fail states | Explore and inhabit, no combat |
| Stripping away game conventions | First-person experience, not first-person shooter |
| What is NOT shown tells the story | The void between moments IS the point |

**Core lesson:** Cut everything that isn't essential. If a moment doesn't earn its place, it doesn't exist. The void between scenes is where the player's imagination does the real work.
