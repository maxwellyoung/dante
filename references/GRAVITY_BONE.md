# Gravity Bone — Reference Brain

> "Gravity Bone is about setup. The little details in the introductory moments are all put in place to set us up, to teach us certain 'truths' about the game world that are reversed in those final moments." — GameDrinkCode

---

## 1. Overview

- **Developer:** Blendo Games (Brendon Chung, solo)
- **Release:** August 2008, Windows
- **Price:** Free (freeware)
- **Engine:** Modified id Tech 2 (Quake 2) — KMQuake II + Lazarus level designer package
- **Runtime:** ~15-20 minutes
- **Full title:** Citizen Abel: Gravity Bone (6th in the Citizen Abel series)
- **Source code:** Open on GitHub (`blendogames/gravitybone`), GNU GPL
- **Awards:** "Best Arthouse Game" — Game Tunnel Special Awards 2008

Four incarnations during one-year development. The first had traditional FPS elements; Chung "kept adding bits and bits of more and more unconventional elements" because he didn't feel comfortable making a standard FPS.

---

## 2. Narrative Structure

### Setting
The fictional city of **Nuevos Aires**, a nondescript South American locale in the country of **Cervidia**.

### Two Missions

**Mission 1:** Infiltrate a fancy party/embassy. Sneak through staff areas, find a suitcase briefing: "Deliver this drink to the man with red hair." Deliver a (contaminated) cocktail to a target. Clean, elegant, Bond-like.

**Mission 2:** New briefing: "Photograph the five birds." Navigate corridors, break padlocks, photograph exotic birds (which explode when photographed). Platforming across pipes and flag poles. Then — a woman reading a book. You press an elevator button. She approaches. She shoots you.

### The Subversion
The game teaches you its "rules" — you are a competent spy, the world is playful and stylish, missions are quirky but manageable. Then it violently breaks those rules. No warning, no boss fight telegraph, no "danger" music. One moment you're pressing an elevator button; the next you're bleeding on the floor.

### The Ending
She shoots you again. You fall backwards off a balcony. In slow motion, first-person, photographs flash before your eyes — moments from your spy's life that were never shown during gameplay. A birthday party. A person. Mundane, human moments. Only in death do you glimpse who Citizen Abel actually was.

### Why It Works
YOU were the spy. YOU completed those mundane tasks. YOU felt competent and safe. The death is YOUR death. The photo montage reveals the character had an entire inner life the game mechanics deliberately hid from you — the game used its own interactivity against you.

## 3. Visual Design

### Engine as Aesthetic
Quake 2's low-polygon limitations become deliberate art direction. Blocky geometry, flat-shaded surfaces, simple textures. The art direction "does a great job of masking the antiquated engine" — or rather, it makes the antiquated engine the point.

### Characters
Blocky, rectangular-headed figures — "cardboard cutout" characters. IGN: "infinitely more interesting to look at and interact with than the frightening Realdolls game players are often forced to contend with in modern FPS's." Stylized graphic design, not failed realism.

### Color
Bold, saturated color blocking. Rooms defined by strong single hues — deep reds, bright greens, warm yellows. Evokes:
- **Mid-century modern graphic design**
- **1960s spy film aesthetics**
- **Saul Bass** — flat geometric shapes, bold color fields, simplified human forms. Same vocabulary as Bass's title sequences for Hitchcock and Preminger.

### Comparisons
Critics compared to Team Fortress 2 and Portal — all three rejected gritty realism for stylized, colorful FPS approaches.

## 4. Level Design

### Mission 1 (The Party)
Exit elevator into bustling social event. Navigate staff-only areas, find furnace room with briefing suitcase, weave through party to deliver drink. Teaches vocabulary: doors, elevators, locked areas, milling NPCs.

### Mission 2 (Birds and Chase)
More complex. Corridors with locked red doors, outdoor ramps, pipes to jump across, flag poles as platforms. Significant verticality — leaps across chasms to balconies, flag poles, air vents.

### The Chase Sequence
After being shot: chase across building interior, crash across banquet table shattering glasses, through tight corridors, ending at balcony edge. Architecture becomes kinetic — calm spaces become frantic escape routes.

### Space Efficiency
Two levels feel much larger and more varied through changes in lighting, color, ceiling height, and contrast between tight corridors and open exteriors.

### Architectural Storytelling
Explicit instructions are rare — "common sense is all you need." Everything relies on inference. The environment IS the narrative.

## 5. Pacing and Rhythm

### Structure as Joke
Setup → punchline.

**The Setup (Mission 1 + first half Mission 2):** Mundane, slightly absurd spy tasks. Light, playful, almost comedic. You learn the "truths" of this world: you are safe, missions are quirky, the game is charming.

**The Rug Pull:** The woman shoots you. No warning.

**The Punchline (after the first gunshot):** Shock of lying motionless. Desperate chase across banquet table. Second shot. Slow backwards fall. Photo montage.

### Humor That Only Works Because It's a Game
> "Humor that is inherently ludic in nature." — GameDrinkCode

The subversion works because the medium taught you to expect player-protagonist safety. Games train you to believe you can't die during "normal gameplay." Gravity Bone uses that training against you.

## 6. Sound Design

### Music
Three songs by **Xavier Cugat and His Orchestra**: "Maria Elena," "Brazil," "Perfidia" — Latin big-band standards from the 1940s-50s.

### Wong Kar-wai Connection
"Maria Elena" and "Perfidia" were used in Wong Kar-wai's *Days of Being Wild* (1990). Chung: "He makes these really beautiful films and I've always wanted to use the same music in a videogame."

### Effect
Languid, romantic, mid-century cocktail lounge elegance. The music makes the violence land harder by contrast. Praised for "the cohesiveness of its striking visual presentation, soundtrack and effects."

---

## 7. Game Design Philosophy

### Short-Form Games
Proved a 15-minute free game could be artistically significant, critically acclaimed, and emotionally resonant.

### Constraints as Creative Fuel
Quake 2 chosen deliberately — "clean and robust," open-source, gives control and freedom. Low-poly look IS the visual language, not a compromise.

### Remixing
Chung's approach: "modding existing works and tinkering and upending traditional expectations... remixing into new shapes." Games that are "experimental but never inaccessible — playful, unruly, and funny."

### Trust the Player
> "I will make something worth mining for meaning, and I will trust you to dig."

No tutorials, no hand-holding. Environment communicates everything.

### Respecting Time
Says what it needs to say and ends. No padding. Brevity is the design.

## 8. Connection to Thirty Flights of Loving

### Shared Universe
- City of **Nuevos Aires**, country of **Cervidia**
- Non-alcoholic drink **"Maple Victory"**
- Bar called **"Bitter Melon"**
- Thirty Flights is a **prequel** — Abel's assassin from Gravity Bone appears as passive NPC

### Evolution of Technique
- **Gravity Bone:** Cinematic moments (photo montage) happen outside interactive sections
- **Thirty Flights:** Jump cuts embedded WITHIN gameplay — more radical, the player never loses control
- Gravity Bone was the seed; Thirty Flights was the flowering

### Citizen Abel Timeline
1. Citizen Abel episodes 1-4 (Quake 2 mods, 1999-2004)
2. Gravity Bone (2008) — 6th in series
3. Thirty Flights of Loving (2012) — 7th in series
4. Quadrilateral Cowboy (2016) — same universe

## 9. Cultural Impact

### Pioneer of "Short Story" Games
Helped establish the template for short-form narrative games prioritizing style, implication, and cinematic technique over length.

### Direct Descendants
- **Thirty Flights of Loving** (2012) — direct evolution
- **Virginia** (2016) — jump cuts, implication-driven
- **Paratopic** (2018) — fragmented, lo-fi, short format

### Proof of Concept
A free, 15-minute game built on a 1997 engine by one person won critical acclaim and influenced a generation. Artistic ambition has nothing to do with budget, runtime, or technological sophistication.

## 10. The Ending — Deep Analysis

### Sequence
1. Complete bird photography. Encounter woman reading book.
2. Press elevator call button. She approaches.
3. **First shot.** No warning, no dialogue, no telegraph.
4. Chase through building. Crash across banquet table, shattering glasses.
5. She reaches balcony/ledge.
6. **Second shot.** Fall backwards.
7. Slow motion fall. First-person — world recedes above you.
8. **Photo montage:** Birthday party. A person. Mundane human moments never shown during gameplay.

### The Design Insight
The falling-backwards-while-memories-flash is a physical manifestation of the game's thesis: **you were always falling, you just didn't know it.** The setup (missions, objectives, game mechanics) was the misdirection. The punchline is mortality.

The game used its own interactivity against you:
- You inhabited a role while the game concealed the person
- You felt competent and safe because games train you to feel that way
- The photo montage reveals a life you never got to live
- YOUR death, not a character's death — interactive medium makes this devastating

### What It Teaches EV
The ending of Gravity Bone works because it weaponizes the gap between **player knowledge** and **character existence**. EV's "three hours to kill" similarly trades on the gap between what the player does (mundane hotel tasks) and what the character feels (existential wonder, melancholy, waiting). The mundane IS the profound — you just have to frame it right.
